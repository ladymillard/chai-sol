Abstract

Paper Summary

The wording of natural language prompts has been shown to influence the performance of large language models (LLMs), yet the role of politeness and tone remains underexplored. In this study, the authors investigate how varying levels of prompt politeness affect model accuracy on multiple-choice questions. They created a dataset of 50 base questions spanning mathematics, science, and history, each rewritten into five tone variants: Very Polite, Polite, Neutral, Rude, and Very Rude, yielding 250 unique prompts. Using ChatGPT 4o, they evaluated responses across these conditions and applied paired sample t-tests to assess statistical significance.

Contrary to expectations, impolite prompts consistently outperformed polite ones, with accuracy ranging from 80.8% for Very Polite prompts to 84.8% for Very Rude prompts. These findings differ from earlier studies that associated rudeness with poorer outcomes, suggesting that newer LLMs may respond differently to tonal variation.

Key Findings

  • Impolite prompts consistently outperformed polite ones across all test runs
  • "Very Polite" and "Polite" tones performed significantly worse than "Neutral," "Rude," and "Very Rude" (all p < 0.05)
  • "Neutral" performed significantly worse than "Very Rude," and "Rude" performed significantly worse than "Very Rude" — a clear gradient where decreasing politeness corresponded to increasing accuracy
  • Polite prompts may introduce ambiguity through indirect phrasing (e.g., "Could you please tell me..."), while curt prompts strip away linguistic padding, giving models clearer intent
  • The authors note: "the politeness phrase is just a string of words to the LLM" — the AI likely reacts to structure, not sentiment
  • Results contradict Yin et al.'s cross-lingual study which found that impolite prompts typically reduced performance, suggesting newer LLMs (GPT-4o) respond differently to tonal variation

Impact & Ethics

Media Coverage & Ethical Note

The paper received worldwide attention and was covered by major news outlets including Reuters, Fox News, Fortune, NDTV (India), ZDNet (France), UN University, and LiveScience.com.

Ethical Note from the authors: "Using insulting or demeaning language in human–AI interaction could have negative effects on user experience and accessibility." The researchers do not recommend that users adopt rude prompting as a practice.
